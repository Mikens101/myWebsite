Recently, I personally experienced this interdisciplinary connection that engineering has on other fields by solving a problem that I was facing during a jazz band rehearsal. During this rehearsal, a new piece of music was passed out and required a specific type of trumpet mute known as a “bucket mute.” Searching through the pile of miscellaneous mutes, I found one bucket mute, however, it did not attach like normal mutes and had metal clips to attach to the trumpet bell. In examining this mute, I realized several flaws; the metal clips would scratch the lacquer of the instrument, it was difficult to quickly attach and remove from the instrument --which was required by the music--, and the overall price was extremely high.

In assessing the problem in front of me, I immediately began designing a new bucket mute that could fit its purpose and be low cost. After conducting some research on this specific type of mute, I discovered that there are not many variations of this insufficient design, and decided to take matters into my own hands. With several prototypes and computer-aided design, I created a functioning model that could be 3D printed; containing hooks that would slide onto the instrument for quick transitions, and mimicking the same dull sound of the original mute. This project exhibits the intersection between engineering and music, especially through its steps of addressing the problem with research, design, prototyping, and improving the end product; similar to that of the engineering process.

The environment is humanity's source of life; it is our responsibility to protect it with our lives. In recent decades, human activity has caused significant carbon buildup in our atmosphere, negatively impacting Earth's climate. Renewable technology such as solar panels, wind turbines, and hydro-electric dams have been developed to minimize our carbon footprint. However, current clean energy harvesters have common deficiencies; they all depend on weather conditions and are unreliable. As conditions continue to worsen, it becomes increasingly dire to find a solution, and I believe that solution lies in the advancement of technology.

If you're like me, you enjoy ordering items from an online store like Amazon. The ultimate convenience: things we want or need arriving at our doorstep with the click of a button. Although this may appear to decrease carbon emissions by minimizing personal vehicle use, it simply passes along the problem, in this case, to Amazon. The plague of convenience.

Currently we have not properly addressed this issue using methods insufficient to combat the rapidly increasing carbon emissions into our atmosphere. Many people thought the introduction of electric cars would significantly decrease carbon emissions. Although this is the right step toward a solution, it just interchanges the plug; instead of a gas pump, an outlet --powered by fossil fuels! As a leading world issue, limiting carbon emissions needs to be focused on industries that are the largest producers of greenhouse gasses.

The root of the problem. Modern technology like cargo ships require a lot of energy to function, and are one of the biggest contributors to carbon emissions; each releasing up to four-hundred million metric tons of carbon dioxide annually. Previous presidents have taken action against fossil fuels, imposing policies to restrict use of these major carbon emitters; however, the world economy cannot survive without the shipping industry as it provides us with essential intercontinental trade. Right now, I believe that we need to focus more on developing technology to provide better alternatives to reduce emissions. We cannot simply shut down fossil fuel use. We must develop effective means to solve its inefficiencies. We must improve technology, not get rid of it.

With the ramifications of climate change heading toward us at full speed, I believe that the most effective way to solve this problem is from its roots --advancing technology to be more efficient and harvest clean energy to use-- and that it takes everyone.
